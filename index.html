Rules and Examples


Rule 1) All Normal Mine Sweeper rules apply. This is an alternate way to play Mine Sweeper that I created.

Mine Sweeper rules are that there are some number of mines on the puzzle board. Each cell contains a clue value
that tells you exactly how many mines are in the surrounding 8 cells (3 in a corner, 5 on a wall).

In normal Mine Sweeper, you are clicking on cells to reveal their clue, while avoiding clicking on cells
with a mine. My puzzle is static. Your job here, is not to click on cells to reveal them, but to mark the cells
without any value written in them as either safe (x) or with a flag (f)


Rule 2) All safe cells with a clue of 0, 1, 5, or 6 have been revealed. Clues of 7 and 8 are impossible due to Rule 4.


Rule 3) All Gray Flags have been revealed. A Gray Flag is a cell that contains a mine and has a clue value of 1.
These cells are Gray and contain the letter F. They count towards the total number of mines on the grid.


Rule 4) All cells that contain mines have clue values of either 1, 2, or 3.


Rule 5) All safe cells are revealed if its clue number minus the number of Gray Flags it can see is 0 or 1.


Rule 6) Some cells have nothing written in them, but have the color Yellow or Orange. These are Color Clues.
A Color Clue tells you the clue value of that cell. Yellow = 2, Orange = 3. This tells you how many mines are
in the surrounding 8 cells. It does NOT tell you if the cell itself contains a mine or not.

Technically Color Clue 4 exists too, but since no mine can ever have a clue of 4, the number 4 has been
written into the cell for your convenience.

Leader Board Rules


First, I am not implementing any kind of bot detection, because I just want to get this website live. I'll check the leader board every now and then, but as far as it goes, this is a small puzzle website for these puzzles, don't be a jerk. Solve them legitimately. That said, in order to qualify for the leader board:

1. You cannot use the reset progress button. You start when you start.

2. Once all cells are filled in, you click the check solution button. If you have a mistake, there is an option to reveal all of the incorrect cells. If you use this option, you are disqualified from the leader board for that puzzle.


Information about Data usage


I believe in complete transparancy for how I am using data and your computer's storage. Below is a complete list of everything that is getting saved to your local storage.

1. I save a list of puzzles you've solved. This is exclusivly to update the thumbnails in the puzzle selection menu. I thought it would look cool to update those as you solve them.

2. For the purpose of the leader boards, I am using your local storage to keep track of how long it takes you to solve a puzzle, and your progress in that puzzle. Like I said, I'm not going to implement any bot detection for cheating. This is just a friendly puzzle site. Please don't cheat.

3. I am maintaining a google sheet for the leader boards. I store your 3 letter initals and your completion time when you submit your score.

There are 3 ways we can solve the final mine for this puzzle. They are all important for solving harder puzzles.

I've highlighted 3 cells, an x, a 1, and an F.

The x) Look at rule 5. This x can see 2 gray flags and 1 regular flag. Since this cell contains an x, the clue value here must be a 2, 3, or 4. It can't be a 2 because it can see 3 flags already. What about a 3? Apply the formula is rule 5.

Clue - Gray Flags <= 1 | 3 - 2 <= 1

This is True, and therefore the cell is required to be revealed to the player.
Since this cell is not revealed, the clue here cannot be a 3. Therefore, it must be a 4. Making the only open space it can see an x. This is what I meant earlier that every cell that contains an x must see at least 2 non-gray flags.

The 1) Assume the cell directly above the 1 is the mine. That would make the cell to the upper right of the 1 an x. Now consider the mine we just created. How many cells can it see that contain mines? The answer is 1. That would make this mine a gray flag. Gray flags, as per rule 3, must be revealed to the player. Since this cell isn't revealed to the player, it cannot be a mine. Therefore the cell directly above the 1, is an x.

The F) Bcause of rule 3, gray flags must see exactly 1 mine. This gray flag currently sees no mines, but it can see 1 open space. That forces that open space to be a mine. Which then we would either use the 1 and normal mine sweeper logic, or we could count the number of flags we've placed, to find that we have now placed 15 flags, which completes the puzzle.

So, as you can see, the rules create a set of cells which are required to be revealed to the player.
You can use these rules, and the fact that cells do not meet the requirements within these rules, to solve puzzles.

In fact, that's how I create these puzzles. My generator creates a random puzzle grid that fits all of the rules for mine placement (forcing all mines to see 1, 2 or 3 other mines, and removing any that see 0 mines).
Then, I create the puzzle by finding all of the cells that are forced to be revealed by these rules.

From there, I use mine sweeper logic combined with the methods described in the above solve example, to see just how far I can solve the puzzle. Then from there, the generator creates color clues until the puzzle is solvable.

Lastly, I do one final check for solvability, and I rank the puzzle based on how many logical steps it takes to solve it. By just using the steps shown in the above example and normal mine sweeper logic, you should be able to solve pretty much all of the Easy and Medium puzzles. The Hard puzzles will usually involve some contradition searching, where you guess if a cell is an x or an f, and see if the puzzle results in any contradictions.
The Expert puzzles always involve contradiction searches, and the hardest ones involve searching through disconnected regions for contradictions.

For example, region searching would look like: Assume you have 2 disconnected regions. Region 1 has at least 2 mines and at most 4 mines. Region 2 has at least 3 mines and at most 5 mines. The puzzle has only 6 mines left to place. Therefore, any soluton that would place 4 mines in Region 1 or 5 mines in Region 2 must be incorrect.

My puzzle generator creates 98 puzzles at a time. That is 14 weeks of puzzles. It then orders them based on difficulty. So, Day 1 has the easiest puzzle. Day 8 has the second easiest. Monday of week 14 has the 14th easiest puzzle. Tuesday of week 1 has the 15th easiest puzzle. The pattern breaks on Sundays. The 14 hardest puzzles are interlaced.
Sunday of Week 1 has the 14th hardest puzzle. Sunday of Week 2 has the 7th hardest puzzle. Sunday of Week 3 has the 13th hardest puzzle, and so on. So, Sunday of Week 14 is the hardest puzzle of the 98 puzzles in that group.

The start of Week 1 for the first 98 puzzles was on January 5th 2026. So, Sunday, April 12th 2026 will be the hardest puzzle of the group. Monday, April 13th 2026, will be the easiest puzzle of puzzle group 2.
